## 1. Executive Summary

Cardano's treasury governance has worked exactly as designed and that is precisely the problem. CIP-1694 built a system where DReps vote individually on every withdrawal, gated by an annual Net Change Limit and a Constitutional Committee constitutionality check. In its first eighteen months of full operation, that system has proven it can say no: it killed the Cardano Foundation's $7.8M Summit 2026 budget by roughly 1.5 percentage points, produced a mixed 6-pass/3-fail split on IOG's nine-proposal infrastructure push, and forced the abrupt transfer and partial cancellation of Project Catalyst — a program that had distributed over $150M across 2,200+ projects since 2020. Defenders call this fiscal discipline finally functioning. The author of "Reforming Treasury Governance," DRep AtlasHub, calls it a "funding impasse" and a "gridlock" that has "invited toxicity" and put rival domains into a "perceived competition for scarce resources" serious enough to endanger Cardano's stability.

Now that the actual on-chain text is available (gov_action1t5ap7etluvct2x69nj5mfqhurgakuvekyflcsrv2qkrhrs378n3qq3z2c22), the proposal is more concrete and considerably more coherent than the bundled-buzzwords pattern this report's first draft had to infer from press coverage alone. It is an Info Action — explicitly non-binding, explicitly "basic ideas for discussion," explicitly disclaiming that implementation is in scope. Its actual architecture has four moving parts: (1) a reframing of the NCL as a vague approximation of a budget rather than the budget itself; (2) a mandatory thematic budget with eight named domains (Core Protocol, Developer Tooling, Research, Commercial Growth, Administration, Community/Marketing, Other Strategic/Public-Good, and a Functional Reserve) that DReps vote on as percentage allocations *before* any individual project is considered; (3) a "strategic entity," elected for a fixed term, responsible for preparing and proposing that budget; and (4) an "expert commission," also elected for a fixed term, responsible for evaluating and selecting individual projects within the budget DReps already approved. The explicit, named voting sequence is NCL → budget (overall amount, structure, percentages) → final treasury withdrawal (the entity/commission's selected project list within that structure) — and the proposal's central claim is that DReps should never again vote a single project's funding against another project's funding in a head-to-head, winner-take-the-NCL contest.

This is a meaningfully better-designed proposal than "create a council and trust it." Sequencing the vote so DReps approve the *shape* of spending (percentage to research vs. marketing vs. infrastructure) before anyone evaluates *which* projects fill those buckets is a real, importable idea — it is structurally similar to Arbitrum's category-bounded Foundation budget and to a portfolio-constraint version of the Option 4 "Strategic Planning Council" this report independently proposed in its first draft. But it is still an Info Action with no binding force, no defined election mechanics for either the entity or the commission, no disclosed safeguards against capture, no sunset clause, and — most importantly for any DRep weighing how to vote — it asks for an *implied mandate to proceed toward implementation* without DReps actually being asked to ratify any specific charter, term length, eligibility rule, or override mechanism. That gap between "good idea, badly specified" and "ready to implement" is the crux of this report's recommendation.

My recommendation is **not** to vote this proposal up or down as a monolith and is **not** to wait passively for a future "implementation" action to fill in the blanks DReps have no power to amend once a precedent of "the community already endorsed this direction" exists. It is to treat the Info Action as exactly what it says it is — an invitation to public discussion — and respond with a counter-sequence: (1) separately ratify the budget-sequencing idea (NCL → thematic budget → project selection) as a process reform, since it requires no new standing body and directly fixes the bundling and head-to-head-competition problems AtlasHub correctly diagnoses; (2) defer creation of the "strategic entity" and "expert commission" specifically, pending a fully-specified follow-up proposal that answers who can run, how seats are elected, what conflicts of interest are disclosed, what term length and sunset clause apply, and what recourse DReps retain if either body underperforms; and (3) use the thematic-budget categories as a genuine starting taxonomy, since AtlasHub's eight-domain breakdown is a reasonable, comparator-consistent structure that should not be thrown out along with the unresolved entity-and-commission questions. Section 5 lays this out as a ranked, conservative-to-ambitious ladder with implementation paths, safeguards, and sunset mechanics for each rung.

## 2. Current Problems (Detailed)

### 2.1 How the system actually works today

Cardano's on-chain treasury governance, as constituted under CIP-1694 and the ratified Constitution (85% approval, February 2025), runs through a small number of mechanisms:

- **Net Change Limit (NCL):** A constitutionally-required Info Action (TREASURY-01a) that DReps must approve, by simple majority of active voting stake, before any Treasury Withdrawal actions for that period can be validly ratified. The 2025 NCL was set at 350 million ADA (covering epochs 532–604), later extended by 8 epochs to maintain continuity into February 2026. A subsequent Info Action proposed 300M ADA for 2025 and 250M ADA for 2026. This is a ceiling, not a budget — it caps total withdrawals but allocates nothing.
- **Treasury Withdrawal actions:** Individual governance actions, each requiring roughly 67% DRep approval (the exact published threshold for treasury withdrawals is 0.67) plus Constitutional Committee constitutionality sign-off, subject to a one-epoch enactment delay. These are submitted ad hoc by any ADA holder, though in practice the overwhelming majority come from IOG, the Cardano Foundation, EMURGO, Intersect-administered budget cycles, and a long tail of independent proposers (Amaru, Dingo, Pebble/Gerolamo, DeFi Liquidity, etc.).
- **The Constitutional Committee (CC):** A seven-member elected body (Cardano Curia among current members) that checks constitutionality, not merit or strategic fit. It does not set priorities or evaluate budget quality.
- **Intersect's Budget Process Framework:** A 2026 innovation, not part of the original CIP-1694 design — a five-stage, off-chain-first workflow (Hydra-voting tool feedback, off-chain temperature check, formal proposal, on-chain Treasury Withdrawal) intended to filter and consolidate proposals before they hit the chain.
- **The Cardano Foundation's 2026 review framework:** Another 2026 add-on — dual independent reviewers scoring proposals against five strategic pillars (infrastructure/research, adoption/utility, governance, community growth, ecosystem sustainability) and against the "Cardano 2030 Vision," with proposals below 67% off-chain support filtered out before reaching on-chain votes.

Notice what this list tells you: the constitutional core (NCL + per-proposal DRep vote + CC constitutionality check) has not changed since ratification. Everything that looks like strategic triage — Intersect's process, the Foundation's scoring rubric, the Cardano 2030 Vision itself — has been bolted on informally, off-chain, by individual entities, in direct response to 2025–2026 pain. That is the strongest evidence that the system as constitutionally specified is under-equipped for the volume and complexity it now has to handle, and it is also exactly the gap "Reforming Treasury Governance" is almost certainly trying to formalize.

### 2.2 The real pain points, with data

**Volume and proposal fatigue.** By mid-2026 Cardano was running treasury governance "continuously rather than episodically," in Intersect's own description — multiple concurrent Treasury Withdrawal actions, NCL extensions, budget framework Info Actions, and constitutional refinements running in parallel every epoch. IOG alone submitted nine treasury proposals in a single push (spanning consensus scaling, smart contract tooling, developer experience, formal verification, and more); six passed, three failed (Pogun, Blockfrost, and a Layer-2 scaling proposal), and a separate, larger research budget remained at roughly 13% support days before its deadline. For a DRep — especially a part-time, non-professionalized one — that is a lot of independent technical and financial due diligence to perform per epoch, with no shared scoring infrastructure until the Foundation built one informally in April 2026.

**Strategic incoherence at the top, and a genuine resolution worth noting.** The starkest 2026 example: IOG's "Cardano Vision 2026: Human Centred, Scalable, Post Quantum Secure" research initiative — covering quantum-resistant cryptography and Leios scaling work, arguably some of the most technically consequential R&D on Cardano's roadmap — was, per contemporaneous press coverage, tracking toward roughly 87% DRep opposition in its initial bundled form ahead of an early-June deadline. **Correction from the first draft of this report:** RCADA's published on-chain voting record shows that by the time discrete governance actions were cast — "IO: Cardano Upgrades" (Yes, May 23), "IO: Consensus Initiative (Leios)" (Yes, May 23), and "Cardano Vision 2026: Human Centred, Scalable, Post Quantum Secure - IO Research" (Yes, June 4) — these had been **split into separate, narrower asks**, and passed. This is in fact the single clearest piece of evidence in the entire 2025–2026 record that AtlasHub's diagnosis of "no isolated treasury withdrawals for individual projects... voted against one another" is solvable today, without a new entity, simply by splitting bundled mega-proposals into separately-judgeable pieces — exactly proposition [f]/[g] in the actual Info Action text, and exactly what this report's Option 1 (anti-bundling rule) independently recommends. Whatever near-rejection happened at ~87% opposition was real, but it was a symptom of bundling, and unbundling fixed it without anyone needing new standing authority. That is a meaningfully different and more optimistic data point than initial press coverage suggested, and it should inform how much weight DReps put on the "we need a strategic entity to prevent this" argument versus the "we need a no-bundling rule" argument — the evidence so far favors the latter being sufficient on its own in at least this case.

**A genuinely unresolved counter-example, however.** Not every proposal in this period had as clean a resolution. IOG's nine-proposal infrastructure push split roughly 6-pass/3-fail (with Pogun, Blockfrost's maintenance/indexing ask, and IO & Midgard Labs' L2 scalability initiative among those RCADA recorded as No or Abstain rather than Yes), and proposals like "Cardano dOSPO and OMF Program" and "Rare Evo and Dev Gov Day 2026" drew Abstain votes from RCADA specifically, consistent with their stated policy of abstaining on weak-process proposals rather than rejecting the underlying intent. This is the more honest middle ground: bundling-and-splitting explains some of 2026's friction, but not all of it — some proposals genuinely failed or stalled on their merits or on process-quality grounds independent of bundling, which is exactly the kind of judgment call AtlasHub's proposal argues individual DReps are ill-equipped to make at volume across unrelated technical domains.

**Strategic drift and bundling problems, the chronic version.** The Cardano Foundation's own Constitutional Committee meeting notes flag this directly: the Pebble/Gerolamo proposal (a TypeScript smart-contract language bundled with a light-node implementation) drew explicit internal concern over "the efficacy of bundled infrastructure" and personnel-allocation risk, sitting at 41% approval at one snapshot, well below threshold — though it's worth noting RCADA's own record shows a Yes vote on a later-dated "Pebble + Gerolamo - HLabs 2026 Budget" action (April 26) and a separate Yes on "Pebble & Ecosystem maintenance: TypeScript core of Cardano" (May 20), suggesting this too was eventually resubmitted in revised or split form. Bundling unrelated technical initiatives into a single up-or-down vote remains a recurring structural symptom of a system with no pre-vote portfolio-construction step — proposers have an incentive to attach a weak initiative to a strong one rather than submit two clean, separately-judgeable asks, even when the eventual fix (as with Vision 2026 and apparently Pebble/Gerolamo) is simply to split and resubmit.

**The Summit failure as a case study in threshold brittleness.** The Cardano Foundation's Summit 2026 proposal — even after a 22% budget cut, decoupling from EMURGO's TOKEN2049 sponsorship, and increased internal cost-absorption — received 64.61–65.21% DRep support (reports vary slightly by snapshot) against a 66.67% supermajority threshold, failing by roughly 1.5 percentage points; RCADA itself recorded a No vote on this action (April 29). The Foundation publicly and gracefully accepted the result. But the episode reveals two separate problems entangled as one: (a) a strategic question — should Cardano fund a flagship marketing/enterprise-outreach event at all in a depressed-ADA-price environment — and (b) a budget-execution question — was *this specific* $7.8M ask reasonable. A binary vote forces DReps to answer both simultaneously, and a near-miss on a supermajority threshold produces an outcome (zero Summit) that may not reflect what a majority actually wanted (a cheaper Summit), simply because there's no intermediate mechanism. Notably, this is also a category-level question AtlasHub's thematic-budget proposal would handle differently: under an eight-domain budget, "Community, Education and Marketing" would receive a *percentage allocation* DReps vote on once, and the Summit would then compete only against other marketing-category asks for that already-approved pool — not against infrastructure, research, or commercial-growth proposals in an undifferentiated up-or-down contest. Whether that would have changed the Summit's specific outcome is unknowable, but it is a real structural difference worth weighing on its own merits.

**Catalyst transition as a treasury-continuity failure.** Project Catalyst — Cardano's longest-running treasury-funded program, having distributed over $150M across more than 2,200 projects since inception — was abruptly transitioned from IOG stewardship to Cardano Foundation management in early 2026, with Fund15's already-published 18.5M ADA + 250,000 USDM budget returned to the treasury and Fund15/Fund16 effectively cancelled mid-cycle. Hundreds of applicants who had spent months preparing proposals were left without a voting timeline. This is exactly the kind of outcome a "strategic entity with continuity mandate" argument points to: the absence of an institutional home with multi-year accountability for a flagship program meant that an internal "Pentad" reset (IOG, EMURGO, Cardano Foundation, Intersect, Midnight Foundation streamlining their relationship) could simply orphan a program serving thousands of community builders, with the only consolation being a roughly 75–80% DRep approval of the *transition itself* after the fact (RCADA recorded Yes on "Approve Cardano Foundation as New Managing Entity of Project Catalyst," April 6) — DReps were asked to ratify a fait accompli, not to choose among options for Catalyst's future before the disruption happened.

**A pattern worth flagging: RCADA's own Abstain rate on the exact class of proposal this Info Action belongs to.** Looking specifically at how one transparent, methodology-driven DRep has handled the run-up to "Reforming Treasury Governance," the pattern is notable: RCADA recorded **Abstain** on "Net Change Limit (Epoch 613 to Epoch 713)" (Feb 3), **Abstain** again on "Net Change Limit of 300 Million ADA for Epochs 613–713" (Mar 8), **Abstain** on "Cardano Budget Process Framework (facilitated by Intersect)" (Mar 22), **Abstain** on "CARDANO BLOCKCHAIN ECOSYSTEM CONSTITUTION v2.4" (Jan 18), and **Abstain** on "Cardano 2030: Vision, Mission, Strategy Framework and KPIs" (Jan 12) — five consecutive Abstain votes on exactly the structural, process-defining, non-binding-signal class of Info Action that "Reforming Treasury Governance" itself belongs to. That is not noise; per RCADA's own published policy, Abstain is reserved specifically for cases where "an Info action is being used to imply endorsement without accountability" or where "intent... is reasonable but execution or process quality is weak."

**Centralization risk running in both directions.** It would be a mistake to read all of this as "DReps are too powerful, founders too weak." The Foundation's January 2026 decision to delegate an additional 220M ADA to eleven hand-picked DReps (bringing its total delegation to 360M ADA) is itself a centralization vector: a single founding entity can materially move outcomes by choosing which DReps to fund, and there is no on-chain transparency requirement forcing other large ADA holders to disclose similar arrangements. Meanwhile, the "Pentad" — IOG, EMURGO, Cardano Foundation, Intersect, Midnight Foundation — coordinating jointly on proposals like the 70M ADA Critical Integrations Budget creates the opposite risk: a handful of founding-era organizations presenting DReps with consolidated, take-it-or-leave-it asks that are harder to amend than they would be if submitted independently. Any reform that creates a new "strategic entity" or "expert commission" has to reckon with the fact that Cardano already has a centralization problem on *both* the proposer side (Pentad coordination) and the voter side (concentrated DRep delegation) before adding a third locus of concentrated influence.

**Turnout and engagement.** Reported DRep participation on individual actions varies enormously — from roughly 41% (Pebble/Gerolamo, failing) to over 85% (the year-end Critical Integrations/Dune-Pyth vote) to near-unanimous CC sign-off on routine items. There is no single authoritative "average turnout" figure published in a form I can verify, and I would flag any specific aggregate percentage you see quoted elsewhere as needing a primary-source check against gov.tools or adastat.net's own analytics rather than press summaries.

## 3. Analysis of the Submitted Proposal

This section is now built directly on the literal on-chain text (gov_action1t5ap7etluvct2x69nj5mfqhurgakuvekyflcsrv2qkrhrs378n3qq3z2c22), authored by DRep AtlasHub — self-described as "a small DRep, committed to Cardano since 2019. No drama, no FUD, no 'social' media." That framing matters: this is not a Pentad-coordinated, founding-entity proposal of the kind Section 2 flags as a centralization risk. It is an individual DRep's diagnosis, submitted as an Info Action explicitly disclaiming binding force — "an implementation will require further refinements and amendments to the current process as well as the constitution," and "these basic ideas may freely be adopted, extended or modified for concrete implementation which is beyond scope here." Read in good faith, this is closer to a structured discussion paper than a power grab. The analysis below evaluates it on those terms while still applying the same scrutiny any structural reform deserves.

### 3.1 What the proposal actually says

The abstract is candid about the stakes: treasury governance is "in a poor state," there is "a funding impasse," and the existing governance system only "indirectly addresses" treasury governance, leaving it "barely supported." The motivation section goes further, describing "significant dysfunctionalities," a "perceived competition for scarce resources" between different domains, "hostility and detrimental conflict," "ill will and strife that harm the Cardano ecosystem, endangering its existence," and "gridlock over treasury spending between different positions and coalitions." This is strong language for an Info Action, and it tracks closely with the real 2026 record this report's Section 2 documents — the Summit failure, the bundled-then-split IOG mega-proposals, the Catalyst transition chaos.

The proposal's nine "Basic Propositions" [a]–[i] build a specific architecture, not just a sentiment:

- **[a]–[b]** assert treasury governance needs "a clear direction, a coherent strategy and a specific roadmap," and that this must be "explicitly addressed and supported by the governance system and the constitution" — i.e., this is framed from the outset as requiring constitutional change, not merely a process tweak.
- **[c]** makes an important and correct technical point: the NCL is "at best a vague initial approximation for the concept of a budget," not a budget itself, and "a collection of incoherent treasury withdrawals" within that ceiling is "not enough for effective treasury governance." This is the proposal's most defensible claim and the one with the least controversy attached.
- **[d]–[e]** call for "a dedicated budget for investing" that includes "the strategic direction and the roadmap," structured thematically so that "various important domains exist side by side" and "none of these domains can be deferred in favor of another."
- **[f]–[g]** are the structural core: "no isolated treasury withdrawals for individual projects must be put on a vote against one another," and DReps should "vote on dedicated and balanced budgets, not on the funding of individual projects" — explicitly justified on cognitive-load grounds ("few DReps are experts in even a single domain, hardly any are competent in multiple domains").
- **[h]** introduces the **strategic entity**: "responsible for preparing, proposing and implementing a budget," "appointed by a vote for a fixed term."
- **[i]** introduces the **expert commission**: "responsible for the evaluation and selection of projects that best meet the budgetary requirements," also "appointed by a vote for a fixed term."

The proposed **process** is then explicit and sequential: NCL vote → budget vote (overall amount, thematic structure, and percentage allocations across domains, with **no individual projects considered at this stage**) → project application window → final treasury withdrawal vote (the entity/commission's selected project list, structured under the already-approved budget). The proposed **budget structure** is a concrete eight-category taxonomy: (1) Core Protocol and Infrastructure, (2) Developer Tooling and Technical Resources, (3) Research and Innovation, (4) Commercial Ecosystem Growth, (5) Administration and Operational Support, (6) Community, Education and Marketing, (7) Other Strategic or Public-Good Initiatives, and (8) a Functional Reserve "directly accessible under predefined conditions" for emergencies.

### 3.2 Strengths

**The diagnosis is largely correct and well-evidenced by the real 2026 record.** Proposition [g]'s claim that DReps are forced into head-to-head comparisons across domains they aren't equipped to judge maps precisely onto the documented pain in Section 2: Pebble/Gerolamo bundling a TypeScript language against a light-node implementation, the Summit competing implicitly against infrastructure and research asks for the same undifferentiated NCL pool, and IOG's nine simultaneous proposals spanning consensus engineering, developer tooling, and formal verification landing on individual DReps' plates in the same window. A DRep voting competently on a Plutus-performance proposal is not thereby qualified to assess a conference-marketing budget or a post-quantum cryptography research plan, and the current system asks every DRep to do exactly that, repeatedly, with no division of labor.

**Sequencing the vote to separate "shape of spending" from "which projects fill it" is a genuinely importable structural fix**, not just rhetoric. This is the single most concrete and actionable idea in the proposal, and it has real precedent: Arbitrum's Foundation operates within DAO-approved spending *categories*, not a category-blind aggregate; Cosmos chains debate community-pool *parameters* before specific spend proposals are submitted against that pool. AtlasHub's version — DReps vote on percentage allocations across eight named domains before any project is named — is arguably cleaner than either comparator, because it makes the category-level trade-offs explicit and votable rather than implicit in whoever happens to win an aggregate-budget negotiation.

**The Functional Reserve [category 8] directly answers a gap this report's first draft also flagged independently** (the community's own prior call for a standing emergency/bug-fix reserve, separate from the main NCL-gated pool) — convergent evidence from two independent sources that this is a real, recognized gap rather than a speculative concern.

**The proposal is honest about its own incompleteness.** Unlike a proposal that bundles a fully-specified entity-and-commission charter into a single ratification vote, this one explicitly defers implementation detail — "beyond scope here" — and invites "all members of the Cardano ecosystem" to "collaborate." That is the correct posture for an Info Action introducing a structural idea, and it is meaningfully different from, and better than, a proposal that asks for blank-check authority up front.

### 3.3 Weaknesses, vagueness, and risks

**The entity and commission are each one sentence long, and that sentence answers none of the questions that actually determine whether they're safe.** Proposition [h] says the strategic entity is "appointed by a vote for a fixed term" — it does not say who is eligible to run, what the term length is, whether re-election is permitted (and if so, how many times), what disclosure or conflict-of-interest rules apply, what recourse DReps have if the entity underperforms before its term ends, or — critically — whether "appointed by a vote" means a DRep vote, a separate election open to all ADA holders, or something else. Proposition [i] gives the expert commission identical treatment with identical gaps. This is not a minor omission. Every centralization risk this report's earlier comparative research surfaced — Arbitrum's Foundation discretion outpacing DAO oversight, MakerDAO's SubDAO architecture quietly recentralizing around its chief designer's choices, Optimism's Citizens' House facing unclear renewal rules for its own badgeholder seats — traces back to exactly these unanswered questions in *those* systems' founding documents. Ratifying [h] and [i] as written would be ratifying the *concept* of an entity and a commission while leaving every actual safeguard to be decided later, by parties not yet named, through a process not yet specified.

**"Implementing a budget" in [h] is a meaningfully larger grant of authority than "preparing and proposing" one, and the proposal doesn't distinguish them.** Preparing and proposing a budget that DReps then vote on is categorically different from *implementing* it — implementation implies some degree of execution authority once the budget framework passes, which starts to look less like Optimism's veto-only Citizens' House and more like Arbitrum's Foundation, which executes its DAO-approved budget with day-to-day discretion the DAO does not re-vote on line by line. The proposal does not say which of these AtlasHub intends, and DReps evaluating this Info Action should not assume the more conservative reading is the one that will appear in a future implementation action.

**No disclosed safeguard against the entity and commission being drawn from, or captured by, the same small set of organizations already coordinating treasury proposals.** The proposal is silent on eligibility restrictions. Given that IOG, the Cardano Foundation, EMURGO, Intersect, and the Midnight Foundation already coordinate jointly on proposals (the "Pentad" pattern documented in Section 2) and already possess the deepest technical expertise to plausibly staff both bodies, an unrestricted "appointed by a vote" process could simply formalize their existing informal influence into two permanent, constitutionally-recognized seats — without that outcome ever being explicitly debated or chosen.

**No sunset clause, no defined review cadence, no explicit dissolution mechanism.** "Appointed... for a fixed term" tells DReps the term ends, but says nothing about whether the entity or commission is automatically re-created afterward, whether DReps must affirmatively re-ratify the underlying structure (not just re-elect new seat-holders), or whether the community retains a clean, low-friction path to dissolve the bodies entirely if the experiment fails. Every comparable system that has tried something like this — Optimism's Citizens' House, MakerDAO's SubDAOs — built in real review mechanisms (RetroPGF's own pause through 2026 is itself an example of a built-in capacity to halt and reassess) precisely because standing bodies are empirically hard to unwind once created, even when nominally term-limited, because of the soft authority and procedural inertia they accumulate.

**The eight-category budget structure, while a reasonable starting taxonomy, is itself a strategic choice that deserves separate scrutiny, not a rubber stamp inside the same vote as the entity/commission question.** Is "Commercial Ecosystem Growth" meaningfully distinct from "Other Strategic or Public-Good Initiatives" in a way that won't recreate exactly the bundling/boundary disputes the proposal is trying to eliminate? Should "Administration and Operational Support" — funding for "treasury administration... and budget coordination" — be capped at some maximum percentage to prevent the apparatus that runs the budget process from quietly growing its own funding share over time, a known failure mode in institutional budgeting generally? The proposal doesn't address this, and DReps voting on the *category structure* should treat it as a distinct decision from the *entity/commission* decision, exactly as proposition [g]'s own logic — don't bundle unrelated decisions into one vote — would suggest they should.

### 3.4 Net assessment

This is a better-reasoned, better-evidenced, and more honestly-scoped proposal than this report's first draft could have inferred from press coverage alone — AtlasHub correctly diagnoses real, documented 2026 pain, proposes a sequencing fix (NCL → budget-by-category → project selection) with genuine cross-DAO precedent, and is transparent that implementation detail remains open. But propositions [h] and [i] — the strategic entity and expert commission — ask DReps to endorse the *concept* of two new standing, fixed-term, elected bodies without specifying eligibility, term length, conflict-of-interest rules, authority boundaries ("preparing and proposing" versus "implementing"), or any dissolution mechanism. That is precisely the category of proposal a process-quality-focused DRep would treat with caution: reasonable intent paired with execution and process-quality gaps, where voting Yes risks implying endorsement of details that aren't yet specified. The correct DRep response is not to reject AtlasHub's diagnosis — it is largely right — but to decouple the sequencing/categorization idea (which is ready to act on) from the entity/commission idea (which is not yet specified well enough to ratify, even as a direction), and to make that decoupling explicit in any vote rationale rather than treating this as a single up-or-down question. Section 5 below builds exactly that decoupling into a ranked set of reform options.

## 4. Lessons from Other DAOs

| DAO / System | Treasury model | What worked | What failed or remains contested | Transferable lesson for Cardano |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Arbitrum DAO** | Foundation manages multi-year operating budget under board discretion within DAO-approved categories; Security Council (12 elected members) handles emergency/security actions only, not strategy | Clean separation of security emergency power from spending discretion; Foundation absorbs "cost center" framing so DAO can evaluate strategy and execution as one package per cycle | DAO oversight is effectively a single aggregate vote per multi-year cycle, not line-item; Foundation spend (~$27.6M projected 2027 opex) runs well ahead of DAO's own protocol revenue (~$23.5M gross profit 2025), drawing recurring "are we funding a deficit" criticism; a 2026 L1 Timelock vulnerability also showed the constitutional-governance layer itself needs continuous security hardening | A standing strategic body needs an explicit, published test for whether its multi-year ask is growing faster than the treasury's own income — Cardano's NCL-as-formula idea is actually a *better* starting safeguard than Arbitrum has, if implemented rigorously |
| **Optimism Collective** | Bicameral: Token House (OP holders/delegates, day-to-day protocol governance) + Citizens' House (reputation-based badgeholders, controls RetroPGF allocation and holds veto over Token House revenue-diversion votes) | Genuine separation of "who controls the money supply decision" from "who decides how public-goods funding gets allocated"; veto-not-control design lets the reputation layer check the token layer without replacing it | RetroPGF — the Citizens' House's signature mechanism — has been **paused through the end of 2026** after persistent difficulty operationalizing "impact," with even top recipients (Protocol Guild) publicly stating allocations don't track impact proportionally; citizenship itself has had unclear renewal/expansion rules | An expert/reputation commission with veto power, not spending power, is the safer copy-target than a body with direct allocation discretion — and even that safer model needs years of iteration and is not yet a solved problem anywhere |
| **MakerDAO → Sky (Endgame)** | Restructuring a single DAO into modular "SubDAOs"/"Stars" (Spark first, more planned) under an "Atlas" constitutional document, with new tokens (SKY, USDS) and aligned voter-committee structures replacing pure delegate voting | Genuine reduction in decision latency for specialized domains (e.g., Spark's lending-market parameters move faster under its own SubDAO than they could under unified Maker governance); explicit acknowledgment that voter apathy required a structural, not just cultural, fix | Three-plus years of disruption to execute; persistent community concern about quiet recentralization around founder Rune Christensen's specific architectural choices; a 2024 vote to simply rebrand back to "Maker" was itself rejected, signaling unresolved community discomfort with the changes; running two governance tokens (MKR/SKY) simultaneously created real confusion and listing complications | Decomposition into specialized sub-bodies can solve latency and depth-of-expertise problems that a single undifferentiated DRep vote genuinely cannot — but the multi-year disruption cost and the durable "is this just recentralization with better branding" question are real costs Cardano should budget for, not hand-wave past, if it goes this route |
| **Gitcoin Grants (Quadratic Funding)** | Donor-driven QF rounds with sybil/collusion-resistant matching (Passport identity scoring, Connection-Oriented Cluster Matching, pairwise adjustment); wound down its no-code Grants Stack product in May 2025 to refocus on the core grants program and a "toolbox" of mechanisms (QF, retroactive, streaming, conviction voting, prediction-market-based evaluation) | Genuinely opens funding competition to broad community preference rather than concentrated capital or insider committees; continuous, public iteration on attack-resistance is itself a governance asset; recent rounds (GG24) combined QF with Seer prediction-market-based evaluation for harder-to-score categories like research repos | Even mature implementations concede their own defenses "mitigate but do not eliminate" sybil and collusion attacks; popularity bias (well-known teams out-attracting equally-impactful unknown ones) persists; QF requires a continuously replenished matching pool from philanthropic or treasury sources, so it doesn't solve where the money comes from, only how it's split once it's there | QF-style mechanisms are a strong fit for *one specific slice* of Cardano's treasury problem — small/medium community and public-goods grants (the Catalyst-replacement layer) — and a poor fit for large infrastructure or research budgets where technical due diligence, not breadth of donor preference, is the right signal |
| **Cosmos Hub / Cosmos ecosystem** | Community Pool funded by a small tax on staking rewards; spent via on-chain governance proposals (parameter-style or text-then-implementation), with each Cosmos chain free to set its own community-tax rate and proposal process independently | Continuous, automatic, modest replenishment (the tax) decouples treasury growth from large one-off allocation fights; inter-chain diversity lets different Cosmos zones experiment with different treasury rules without one model being forced on all of them | Community-pool spend proposals have repeatedly struggled with low voter turnout and the same "single large validator/whale effectively decides" dynamic that liquid-democracy systems like Cardano's are also exposed to; no Cosmos chain has solved strategic-coherence-across-years any better than Cardano has — proposals remain mostly one-off | A small, automatic, continuous revenue stream into a clearly-labeled "public goods" sub-pool (separate from the main NCL-gated treasury) could let Cardano fund the QF/Catalyst-style layer without re-litigating it as a discretionary ask every cycle — worth exploring as a complement, not a replacement, for the NCL mechanism |

**The cross-cutting finding, stated plainly:** every comparable system that has tried to solve "strategic coherence vs. decentralized control" has picked a point on the trade-off curve, not eliminated the trade-off. Arbitrum traded oversight granularity for planning coherence. Optimism traded allocation simplicity for a genuine but still-struggling expertise layer. MakerDAO traded years of disruption and recentralization risk for decision latency. Gitcoin traded a fully solved allocation mechanism for a narrower, donor-preference-shaped one that can't carry large technical budgets. None of these is a template Cardano can adopt wholesale and call solved. They are four different answers to the same unsolved question, and the honest output of comparative research here is a menu of trade-offs, not a discovered best practice.

## 5. Recommended Reform Models (Ranked, Conservative → Ambitious)

### Option 1 (Most conservative): Ratify AtlasHub's NCL→thematic-budget→project sequencing now; defer the entity and commission

**What it is:** Adopt propositions [c] through [g] of the actual "Reforming Treasury Governance" text as a standalone process reform, separable from [h]/[i]: formally recognize that the NCL is a ceiling, not a budget; require a second, distinct DRep vote that allocates percentages across named thematic domains *before* any individual project is considered; and require that final Treasury Withdrawal actions be structured as project lists *within* those already-approved category allocations, never as isolated head-to-head contests across domains. Pair this with a constitutional guardrail — enforced by the Constitutional Committee's existing constitutionality-check role — that bundled, unrelated initiatives (e.g., a programming language plus an unrelated node implementation) must be submitted as separate asks within their respective categories, not combined into one cross-category vote.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Captures the most defensible and best-evidenced part of AtlasHub's diagnosis (propositions [c]–[g]) without ratifying the unspecified parts ([h]–[i]); builds on an already-constitutional mechanism (TREASURY-01a); directly fixes the documented bundling problem (Pebble/Gerolamo) and the documented head-to-head-competition problem (Summit competing implicitly against infrastructure/research for the same pool); the eight-category taxonomy AtlasHub proposes is usable immediately as a starting structure |
| **Cons** | Does not, by itself, solve who triages individual projects within an approved category — that's still left to ad hoc DRep review unless paired with Option 2; a poorly-calibrated category split could itself become a new site of strategic disagreement (e.g., what percentage to Research vs. Commercial Growth) that simply relocates the gridlock AtlasHub describes one level up rather than resolving it; the community has already flagged the need for a separate, rules-based emergency reserve — AtlasHub's category 8 (Functional Reserve) addresses this directly and should be retained even if categories 1–7's percentages are contested |
| **Decentralization impact** | Neutral to positive — removes one source of opaque negotiation and one source of unfair head-to-head competition, adds no new concentrated authority; the category-percentage vote remains a full DRep vote, just held earlier in the sequence |
| **Implementation path** | A single Info Action formally adopting the NCL→category-budget→project sequence and the eight-domain taxonomy (or a community-refined version of it), followed by a Constitution/Guardrails-Script update action (CIP-1694's existing "Update to the Constitution or Proposal Policy" action type) to encode the anti-bundling and category-sequencing rule into the constitutionality check the CC already performs |
| **Safeguards** | Category percentages published with rationale and reviewed every cycle, not fixed permanently; anti-bundling rule includes a defined exception process (e.g., CC can approve a cross-category bundled exception with a stated justification, itself published); Functional Reserve access conditions defined explicitly enough that it cannot become a backdoor around the category-percentage votes |
| **Success metrics / review** | Track (a) rate of proposal resubmission-after-split versus original bundled submission rate, (b) whether category-percentage votes themselves become contentious enough to recreate the original gridlock at one remove; built-in 2-year sunset/re-ratification requirement on both the category list and the percentages |

**A related but distinct idea, not in AtlasHub's text, worth pursuing alongside Option 1:** moving the NCL itself from a politically-negotiated annual figure to a published formula (e.g., a percentage of trailing treasury inflow, modeled on sovereign-wealth-fund spending rules the community has already informally discussed in comparison to Norway's GPFG). AtlasHub's proposition [c] correctly identifies that the NCL is "at best a vague initial approximation" of a budget, but doesn't itself propose fixing *how the NCL number is chosen* — only that it shouldn't be mistaken for the budget. Formalizing the NCL-setting methodology is a clean, low-risk complement to Option 1's category-sequencing reform, addressing a slightly different gap in the same area.

### Option 2: Standing Treasury Triage & Scoring Committee (a fully-specified, recommendation-only version of proposition [i]'s "expert commission")

**What it is:** Adopt the *concept* in proposition [i] — a body "responsible for the evaluation and selection of projects that best meet the budgetary requirements" — but resolve every question AtlasHub's text leaves open, in the conservative direction: seats are community-elected (not founding-entity-appointed), modeled on Optimism's Citizens' House in function; the commission scores and flags proposals for technical soundness, budget viability, and category fit before they go to a DRep vote within the relevant thematic allocation; and it may *recommend* a project list and ranking, but holds **no binding veto, no implementation authority, and no power to allocate funds DReps have not already approved into that category.** This differs from AtlasHub's proposition [i] in one specific, deliberate way: where the original text says the commission is "responsible for the evaluation and selection of projects," this version makes the commission responsible for evaluation and *recommended* selection only — the final Treasury Withdrawal vote ratifying which recommended projects actually receive funds remains a binding DRep vote, not an action the commission can take on its own once a category budget passes.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Directly attacks the proposal-fatigue and due-diligence-burden problem AtlasHub correctly identifies in proposition [g], using the best available precedent (Optimism's veto-not-control split); keeps founding-entity influence checked by making seats elected rather than appointed; lowest-risk version of the "expert commission" idea, since recommendation-only means a captured or mistaken commission can't unilaterally select or fund anything DReps disagree with — closing the specific gap (does "selection" mean binding or advisory?) the literal proposal text leaves open |
| **Cons** | Risk of de facto rubber-stamping if DReps start treating "scored well by the Committee" as sufficient and stop independent review (the documented Optimism/Gitcoin pattern of expertise layers drifting toward unchecked authority over time); election design (who can run, how seats are apportioned across technical/financial/community expertise) is nontrivial and itself a governance design problem AtlasHub's text does not address |
| **Decentralization impact** | Positive if seats are DRep-elected with term limits and disclosed conflicts of interest; negative if seats default to founding-entity nomination (the failure mode to explicitly guard against, and the central gap in proposition [i] as written) |
| **Implementation path** | An Info Action establishing the committee's charter, seat count, election method, term length, and explicit "recommendation only, non-binding" status; could run as a pilot for one full budget cycle (2027) before any further constitutional embedding, since Info Actions don't require constitutional amendment |
| **Safeguards** | Mandatory conflict-of-interest disclosure for any committee member affiliated with a proposing entity (IOG, CF, EMURGO, Intersect, Midnight Foundation, or any of their major delegation recipients); maximum term limits with staggered re-election; published scoring rubric and reasoning for every proposal, not just a yes/no recommendation, so DReps can audit the *logic*, not just trust the *score* |
| **Success metrics / review** | DRep override rate (how often DReps vote against the Committee's recommendation — too low suggests rubber-stamping, a healthy range suggests genuine independent check); time-to-vote reduction per proposal; mandatory sunset and full re-ratification vote after 12 months, explicitly including the option to disband |

### Option 3: NCL-first sequencing as binding annual gate + ring-fenced public-goods sub-pool (Cosmos-style)

**What it is:** Combine Option 1's NCL formalization with a structurally separate, smaller, continuously-replenished sub-pool (a fixed percentage of the NCL, not a separate tax, since Cardano's treasury inflow mechanics differ from Cosmos's staking-reward tax) earmarked specifically for Catalyst-successor-style community/public-goods funding, allocated via quadratic-funding-style mechanics (Gitcoin-style sybil-resistant matching) rather than individual DRep up-or-down votes on every small grant.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Directly restores a continuity-protected home for the kind of small/medium community funding Catalyst used to serve, without re-exposing it to the kind of abrupt entity-reshuffling cancellation that orphaned Fund15/16; QF mechanics are well-suited to exactly this funding size and shape (many small, hard-for-individual-DReps-to-individually-evaluate grants) |
| **Cons** | Importing QF brings Gitcoin's well-documented, still-unsolved sybil/collusion exposure; requires meaningful new tooling (identity verification, matching algorithm, dispute resolution) that doesn't yet exist natively in Cardano's governance stack — likely a multi-quarter build, not a parameter change |
| **Decentralization impact** | Positive for funding access (genuinely opens small-grant funding to broad community preference rather than DRep gatekeeping on every micro-grant) but introduces a new technical attack surface (sybil resistance) that itself becomes a centralization-adjacent design choice (who runs identity verification, who adjudicates collusion disputes) |
| **Implementation path** | Requires (a) an Info Action defining the sub-pool's size and formula as a fixed share of the NCL, (b) a Treasury Withdrawal action funding the sub-pool's smart-contract escrow for a defined period, (c) integration or adaptation of existing open-source QF tooling (Gitcoin's Grants Stack was open-sourced before its May 2025 wind-down and could plausibly be forked/adapted rather than built from scratch) |
| **Safeguards** | Hard cap on sub-pool size as a percentage of NCL (so it cannot silently become the dominant treasury outflow); independent sybil-resistance audit before launch; a published, public dispute-resolution process modeled on Gitcoin's flagging mechanism |
| **Success metrics / review** | Number of unique funded recipients vs. prior Catalyst rounds; measured sybil/collusion incident rate; participant satisfaction survey of both grantees and small donors; mandatory 18-month review with explicit sunset option |

### Option 4: Term-limited, narrowly-mandated Strategic Planning Council (a fully-specified, authority-bounded version of proposition [h]'s "strategic entity")

**What it is:** If the community concludes a standing strategic-planning body is genuinely needed — distinct from Option 2's triage committee — it should resolve proposition [h]'s open questions explicitly, in the conservative direction, rather than ratifying "appointed by a vote for a fixed term" and leaving the rest to a future implementation action. AtlasHub's text says the entity is "responsible for preparing, proposing and implementing a budget" — this version splits that verb list and keeps only the first two: the Council *prepares and proposes* a non-binding, multi-year *roadmap and portfolio framework* (e.g., the category percentages from Option 1, revisited and refined annually) that DReps vote to adopt or reject as a reference document, the way the Cardano 2030 Vision was already adopted (RCADA itself abstained on that vote, consistent with treating non-binding strategic frameworks with caution) as a non-binding strategic reference rather than a binding budget. It deliberately **excludes "implementing"** — execution and disbursement remain governed by ordinary Treasury Withdrawal votes DReps cast directly. Crucially: **no spending authority, no proposal-gatekeeping authority, election (not appointment) of all seats, mandatory disclosure of any affiliation with IOG/CF/EMURGO/Intersect/Midnight Foundation, and a hard sunset clause requiring full re-ratification every single budget cycle (annually), not biennially or longer.**

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | Addresses the genuine strategic-incoherence problem at its root, by giving DReps a portfolio-level reference to vote on once a year instead of re-deriving strategic priorities proposal-by-proposal; precedent already exists in Cardano's own 2030 Vision process, so this is evolution, not invention; explicitly resolves the "preparing/proposing" vs. "implementing" ambiguity in proposition [h] that this report's Section 3 flags as its single largest unanswered question |
| **Cons** | This is the option with the worst current optics, given the documented Pentad-coordination pattern in Section 2 — any version of this, even AtlasHub's individually-authored one, risks being read as a structure later co-opted by coordinated founding-entity influence if eligibility isn't restricted from day one; even with annual re-ratification and elected seats, a standing body inevitably accrues soft authority over time (the Optimism/MakerDAO pattern) that is hard to claw back once granted; "non-binding reference document" bodies have a known failure mode of becoming binding-in-practice once DReps start reflexively deferring to them |
| **Decentralization impact** | Net negative risk unless the safeguards below are non-negotiable; this is the option that most needs DRep-majority (not founding-entity) control over composition to avoid being a recentralization vector wearing a strategy-document costume |
| **Implementation path** | Requires a full constitutional amendment process (Update to the Constitution action type), not just an Info Action, given the standing-body and recurring-mandate nature of what's being created — note that proposition [b] of the original text itself concedes this ("explicitly addressed and supported by the governance system and the constitution"); should be preceded by, not bundled with, Options 1–3, so DReps can evaluate this with real data on whether the lighter-weight reforms already solved most of the pain |
| **Safeguards** | Elected (not appointed) seats with a majority requiring no current affiliation with IOG, EMURGO, Cardano Foundation, Intersect, or Midnight Foundation; explicit, hard-coded "non-binding reference only" status enforced by the constitutionality check (any future proposal citing the Council's framework as binding justification should itself be flagged by the CC); mandatory annual full-body re-election and re-ratification, not staggered terms; an explicit, advertised mechanism for DReps to call a vote dissolving the Council outright at any time, not only at renewal |
| **Success metrics / review** | DRep override rate on proposals the Council's framework would have favored or disfavored; measured reduction (or absence of reduction) in strategic-incoherence incidents like the Vision-2026 near-rejection pattern; community sentiment survey explicitly asking whether the Council is perceived as influencing outcomes beyond its formal non-binding mandate — a "yes" on that question in surveys is itself a trigger for early review |

### Option 5 (Most ambitious — not recommended for the next 12–24 months): Full SubDAO-style decomposition

**What it is:** The MakerDAO/Sky Endgame-style move — decompose Cardano's single unified treasury-governance process into specialized sub-bodies (e.g., an Infrastructure sub-treasury, a Public Goods sub-treasury, a Growth/Marketing sub-treasury), each with its own delegate election, its own budget within an NCL-set ceiling, and faster internal decision cycles than full-DRep votes currently allow.

| | |
|---|---|
| **Pros** | The only option on this list that directly addresses decision *latency* and *depth of domain expertise* at the same time — Spark's experience inside Maker/Sky shows specialized sub-bodies genuinely can move faster and more knowledgeably on domain-specific decisions than a single undifferentiated vote pool |
| **Cons** | MakerDAO's own experience shows this takes multiple years to execute well, with real disruption cost and a durable, still-unresolved community concern about quiet recentralization; Cardano does not currently have the tooling, the precedent within its own constitution, or — most importantly — the evidence that lighter-weight reforms (Options 1–4) have been tried and failed, which any community should require before accepting multi-year disruption |
| **Decentralization impact** | Could go either way depending entirely on execution quality DReps cannot currently evaluate in advance, which is itself the strongest argument against attempting it now |
| **Implementation path** | Would require sequential constitutional amendments defining each sub-body's mandate, election method, and budget-ceiling-within-NCL mechanics — multi-year, multi-CIP undertaking |
| **Safeguards / metrics** | Not applicable at this stage — the right "safeguard" against Option 5 right now is simply not doing it until Options 1–4 have run long enough to produce evidence about whether they were sufficient |

## 6. My Top Recommendation and Implementation Roadmap

**Vote to decouple, not to ratify or reject the Info Action wholesale: support the spirit of propositions [c]–[g] (sequence Options 1 and 2 immediately and in parallel); pilot Option 3 on a smaller scale to address Catalyst's continuity gap specifically; explicitly defer propositions [h] and [i] as written (Option 4) pending a fully-specified follow-up; do not pursue Option 5 this cycle.**

The reasoning is straightforward given everything above. Options 1 and 2 are the only two reforms on this list that (a) require no constitutional amendment, only Info Actions and a Constitution/Guardrails-Script update for the anti-bundling/category-sequencing rule, (b) carry low-to-moderate centralization risk *if* built with the safeguards specified, (c) directly target the two best-evidenced 2025–2026 pain points — bundled/opaque proposals and DRep due-diligence overload — and (d) are reversible within a single budget cycle if they don't work, via their built-in sunset clauses. Notably, Options 1 and 2 also capture nearly everything *defensible* in AtlasHub's actual text without requiring DReps to pre-commit to the specific, unspecified entity-and-commission architecture in propositions [h]–[i] — DReps can vote Yes on the diagnosis and the sequencing fix while withholding judgment on the two new standing bodies until they're fully specified. Option 3 is worth a small, explicitly-bounded pilot because Catalyst's abrupt cancellation left real, documented harm (hundreds of orphaned applicants) that none of the other options address, but it should launch capped and small precisely because its QF tooling is unproven on Cardano specifically. Option 4 — the literal strategic entity proposition [h] asks for, here resolved into a bounded, recommendation-only Council — should be explicitly tabled, not rejected outright, pending two full cycles of data from Options 1–2: if category-sequencing and a recommendation-only triage committee turn out to solve most of the fatigue and bundling problems, the case for a standing strategic body with even non-binding authority weakens considerably, and the community will be voting on a future, fully-specified version of proposition [h] with real evidence rather than the current mix of legitimate diagnosis and unanswered design questions. Option 5 is not appropriate to attempt before the lighter reforms have been tried.

### Roadmap

**Months 0–3:**
- Respond to "Reforming Treasury Governance" (gov_action1t5ap7etluvct2x69nj5mfqhurgakuvekyflcsrv2qkrhrs378n3qq3z2c22) on its own stated terms: it explicitly invites public discussion and disclaims being implementation-ready, so the appropriate DRep response is a published rationale that endorses propositions [a]–[g] in spirit while explicitly flagging [h] and [i] as requiring a separate, fully-specified follow-up action before any DRep should be asked to ratify them — not a simple Yes/No/Abstain with no accompanying reasoning.
- Submit Option 1 as a standalone Info Action: formal adoption of the NCL-is-not-a-budget distinction and the category-percentage sequencing vote, using AtlasHub's eight-domain taxonomy as the starting structure, with the anti-bundling constitutionality rule submitted as a parallel Constitution/Guardrails-Script update action.
- Begin community forum design discussion (Cardano Forum, Governance Hours) on Option 2's committee charter: seat count, election mechanism, conflict-of-interest disclosure rules, and explicit "recommendation-only" constitutional status — modeled openly on Optimism's Citizens' House veto-not-control split, but adapted to "recommend-not-veto" given Cardano's different proposal volume and the desirability of keeping the binding vote unambiguously with DReps.

**Months 3–9:**
- Hold the Option 2 committee's founding election; run its first full triage cycle on a real 2027 budget round, in parallel with normal DRep voting (i.e., DReps see both the Committee's score *and* the proposal, vote as they currently do, and the override rate gets measured from day one).
- Scope and design a small, capped Option 3 pilot specifically as a Catalyst-continuity mechanism — explicitly sized and time-boxed (e.g., a single fixed percentage of the NCL, for one annual cycle), built on adapted open-source QF tooling rather than from-scratch development, with an independent sybil-resistance review before launch.

**Months 9–18:**
- Publish the first full-cycle data set: NCL-formula variance, anti-bundling resubmission rates, Committee override rates, QF-pilot sybil-incident and unique-recipient counts.
- Hold the mandatory re-ratification votes built into each option's sunset clause — this is also the natural point for the community to decide, with real evidence in hand, whether Option 4 (the standing strategic entity) is still needed, needs redesigning, or should be abandoned entirely.

**Months 18–24:**
- If and only if the data shows persistent strategic-incoherence problems (recurring near-rejections, later resolved only by ad hoc splitting, of strategically important but unpopular-in-the-moment budgets) that Options 1–3 demonstrably did not fix, open formal constitutional-amendment discussion on Option 4, with the explicit, non-negotiable safeguards from Section 5's Option 4 discussion as the starting draft, not an aspirational add-on negotiated away under founding-entity pressure.

## 7. Open Questions and Areas Needing Further Community Input

- **Should DReps vote Yes, No, or Abstain on "Reforming Treasury Governance" as currently worded, given that it bundles a well-evidenced diagnosis ([a]–[g]) with two underspecified standing-body proposals ([h]–[i])?** This report argues for treating it as an invitation to discussion rather than a ratification vote, but Info Actions still get a recorded Yes/No/Abstain tally that the community will read as a signal regardless of AtlasHub's own disclaimer that "implementation... is beyond scope here." DReps should think carefully about what each of the three options actually signals here: a Yes risks being read later as having endorsed [h]/[i] specifically; a No risks being read as rejecting the well-evidenced [c]–[g] diagnosis along with it; an Abstain, consistent with how several DReps (including RCADA) have handled structurally similar Info Actions in 2026, signals "good direction, premature to ratify" — but only if paired with a clear public rationale, since a silent Abstain conveys none of that nuance on its own.

- **Who would actually sit on the strategic entity or expert commission, and how would conflicts of interest realistically be enforced** when so much of Cardano's proposal volume already originates from a small set of founding entities (the de facto "Pentad" — IOG, EMURGO, Cardano Foundation, Intersect, Midnight Foundation) that also have the technical expertise needed to evaluate proposals competently? Excluding everyone affiliated with these organizations from either body may also exclude most of the people with the relevant expertise — this tension is real, unresolved in AtlasHub's text, and unresolved in this report's proposed safeguards too; it deserves dedicated community debate before any implementation action is drafted.

- **Is the NCL the right ceiling mechanism at all, or should Cardano consider a Norway-GPFG-style "spend only the real return" rule** that ties withdrawals to a measured percentage of treasury growth rather than a politically negotiated absolute figure each cycle? AtlasHub's proposition [c] correctly diagnoses that the NCL isn't a budget, but doesn't address how the NCL number itself should be set — that remains a separate, open question this report flags in Option 1's discussion, deserving its own dedicated quantitative modeling against actual Cardano treasury inflow data.

- **Are AtlasHub's eight thematic categories the right ones, and at what percentages?** The proposal supplies a taxonomy but no percentages, and no mechanism for resolving disputes about the percentages beyond "DReps vote on it." Given that the Summit/marketing-budget fight, the Vision 2026 research-budget near-rejection, and the Catalyst-transition fallout all map onto different categories in this taxonomy (Community/Marketing, Research/Innovation, and arguably a gap the taxonomy doesn't clearly cover, respectively), the community should explicitly stress-test the eight-category structure against 2025–2026's actual disputes before adopting it, not adopt it first and discover gaps later.

- **What happens to Project Catalyst's displaced applicants and reviewers in the near term**, independent of any longer-term Option 3 pilot or the thematic-budget restructuring this Info Action proposes? Neither AtlasHub's text nor this report's 12–24 month roadmap fully addresses the immediate harm already done by the Fund15/16 cancellation, and the community should consider whether a smaller, faster transitional relief mechanism is needed regardless of how the broader reform question resolves.

- **Should large ADA holders and founding entities be required to disclose delegation arrangements** (like the Cardano Foundation's 360M ADA spread across eleven chosen DReps) with the same transparency expected of proposal funding sources, given that concentrated delegation is a documented influence vector running in parallel with — and potentially shaping the outcome of — any vote on this Info Action and its eventual implementation?

- **Is a 66.67% supermajority threshold for treasury withdrawals calibrated correctly**, given that the Summit 2026 outcome may not reflect what a majority of DReps actually wanted as well as a graduated or conditional-approval mechanism would have? This is a parameter question separate from, but adjacent to, everything proposed in Section 5, and worth its own dedicated community discussion — including whether AtlasHub's category-budget sequencing would make threshold brittleness less consequential by giving near-miss proposals a smaller, category-bounded pool to renegotiate within rather than an all-or-nothing outcome against the whole NCL.
